First

The new reality:

You don’t need to work for all 60/80/100 hours a week. 10-40 hours is enough. If it is not the case, you need to rework the way you are working.

Takedowns

Ignore the real world:

The real world is pessimistic. It judges based on the past experiences, therefore most of the times the answers are negative. “Real world” is just an excuse/ justification of not working towards the goal.

Learning from mistakes is overrated:

Mistakes teach what doesn’t work but how will one find what will work? Expand things that are working; evolution is an analogy where only better things are taken forward not the failures.

Planning is guessing:

Having short plans for the larger goal is important as planning is equivalent to guessing what can happen in the future where future is not in our control – or controlled by many other factors which are not in our hand. However, the margin of error for each plan can be reduced with shorter plans.

Why grow?

It is always the aim of every individual to grow their businesses, however, in the endeavor to grow many unwanted expenses occur. Therefore, finding the right size to the business is important. If the right size is small – it is right. Don’t be ashamed.

Workaholism:

A hard-working employee working for fourteen hours a day is only working for fourteen hours – nothing more than that. It is unnecessary and stupid. Workaholics burnout themselves and is not sustainable in the long run. If they are working all the time, the judgments won’t be sound, as they are always tired.  They complicate the things with mediocre work while the smartest ones are already home by figuring out the fastest solution.

Enough with entrepreneur:

Any business that is profitable is enough. Starting a business only needs an idea, a touch of confidence and a little push – starter. Starting a business is no longer an elite class thing.

Go

Make a dent in the universe:

Feel the need, believe it, work with a sense of urgency, and make a dent. Examples: craigslist, Netflix, etc. had destroyed or made a mark in their industries. You can do it too.

Scratch your own itch:

Solve your own problem, you will find the people who needs the same solution – sell your solution to them. When you are building a product, you need to take multiple tiny decisions. If you are solving your own problem, the light shines on you to make better decisions otherwise it is just shooting in the dark.

Start making something:

What you do is what matters, not what you think or plan to do. Ideas are cheap and plentiful, execution matters. There is negligible value to the idea, in contrast, the product has the highest value.

No time is no excuse:

Squeezing in time for the most important work is always possible. However, if someone says there is no time it means it is not important right now. If you want it bad enough – you have time for it. Everything else is just an excuse to satisfy your ego.

Draw a line in the sand:

Great businesses have a point of view not just a product or service. Strong opinions attract both positive and negative fans. However, the positive fans strongly advocate for the product. We are willing to lose some customers if others are loving our product intensely. This is the “line in the sand”. If you don’t know what to believe everything becomes an argument and is debatable. But when you stand for something, the decisions are obvious.

Live it or leave it

It you are not standing up to what you are claiming, it is not worth claiming. It tarnishes the name forever. Standing for something as “mission statement” isn’t just writing fancy but believing and living up to it.

Outside money is Plan z:

For service-based business we do not require outside funding as seed money. Once we have the outside money as investment, we give control of the product we want to build while we curate the product towards the investors needs. The other reasons for not taking outside money includes investors wanting their money back quickly they usually ignore the sustainability of the product, Spending others money is addictive as it is easier to spend while costing the equity/ shareholding of the company, When we do not have any leverage it is not an ideal time to enter into a financial transaction, We focus on building what investors want instead of what customers want and finally, it is distracting and draining to seek funding – instead of building the product - we waste time in pitch meetings, legal maneuvering, etc. It is just not worth it!

You need less than you think:

Great companies started in garages, yours can too.

Start a business not a startup:

The fact – every business, new or old, face the same tune of the market and same economic rules applies. Revenue in, expenses out. Turn a profit or wind up gone. A business without a path to profit isn’t a business, it’s a hobby.

Building to flip is building to flop:

The exit strategy of a business should be unknown. We should not start a business with the intention of selling to big companies. If we are doing good, we should keep going forward with it. It is rare we find the right match which we are interested in and are doing good. If we build a (good going) company to sell, we sure are going to regret later.

Less mass:

More mass more energy. More mass more difficult to pivot. Mass increases with long term contracts, excess staff, meetings, permanent decisions, thick process, inventory, H/w or s/w lock-ins, office politics, etc.

Progress

Embrace constraints:

Before you sing “not enough” blues, see how far you can get with what you have!

Build half a product, not half assed product:

It is easy to turn great ideas into a crappy product by trying to build all of them at once. It is hard to say no to things, but it is essential. Movie directors cut good scenes to make great movie. Start chopping. Cut merely good things to make a great start.

Start at the epicenter:

When you start anything, there are forces pulling you in all directions. Where to concentrate? Always ask this question, if I do not do this thing, would what I am selling remain the same? Focus all your energy on epicenter and everything else will be built around it.

Ignore the details early on:

Getting infatuated with the details lead to disagreements, meetings and delays. You get lost in things that don’t really matter and change in time. When you feel something is missing, that’s when we need to pay attention.

Making the call is making the progress:

Whenever you can, swap “let’s think about it” for “let’s decide on it.”  It doesn’t matter how much you plan; you will still get some stuff wrong. Therefore, instead of overanalyzing and delaying – act. Longer it takes, less likely to launch.

Be a curator:

The best is the sub-sub-subset of all the possibilities. A curator is involved, making conscious decisions about what should stay and what should go.

Throw less at the problem:

When things are not working, the natural inclination is to throw more at the problem – more people, more time, more resources. Instead, try cutting down. You will be forced to make tough calls – makes product better.

Focus on what won’t change:

The core of the business should be built around what won’t change like Amazon (free shipping, fast delivery, affordable prices). All the design, fashion around the product will change eventually.

Tone is in your fingers:

You do not need the best equipment to get started. Use whatever you got to build the product. Do not go for fancy things – it’s not the gear that matters. The important thing is to play the tune with what you got.

Sell your by-products:

When you make something, you always make something else too. Observe the by-products and sell them.

Launch now:

Stop imagining what’s going to work. It is guessing – which will eventually fail. Working through iterations is the best way. Once the product does what it has to do, launch it.

Productivity

Illusions of agreement:

A hundred people can read the same words but imagine hundred different things. The same thing happens with the reports and documents – illusions of agreement. The best way to make everyone agree/ or see the product, is to make one, at least prototype. That is when we can see intricate details.

Reasons to quit:

Sometimes abandoning what you are working on is the right move. To ensure that you are really working on things that really matter, ask the following questions.

·      Why are you doing this? What is this for? Who benefits? What is the motivation behind it?

·      What’s the problem? Are customers confused? Are you confused? Is something not clear enough? Is there something which was not possible before should be possible now?

·      Is this useful? Are you making something useful or just making something? Cool wears off. Useful never does.

·      Are you adding value? Adding something is easy; adding value is hard.

·      Will this change behavior? Is what you are working on really going to change anything?

·      Is there an easier way? – Problems are simple, we just imagine they require hard solutions.

·      What could you be doing instead? What can’t you do because you are doing this?

·      Is it worth it? Is it worth pulling an all-nighter or can it just be done tomorrow?

Interruption is the enemy of productivity:

If you are constantly staying late and working all nighters or on weekends, it is because you are not getting work done in time. The reason is interruptions. Interruptions break your workday into a series of work moments. Therefore, make sure the work period is unbroken to avoid productivity-zapping interruptions. Set up a rule at work that half of the day is set aside for alone work. A successful alone time means letting go of communications addiction. Give up instant messages, phone calls, email, and meetings. Just shut up and get to work. It’s on you to fight back for your day that is under siege by interruptions.

Meetings are toxic:

Meetings, of all, are the worst interruptions. Avoid them as much as possible or have a clear agenda.

Good enough is fine:

Solving problems with complicated solutions is not needed while an easy workable solution is enough to move on. We can always turn the good enough to great later.

Quick wins:

No one likes to be stuck on endless projects. Build something, move on to next big thing. The quicker it is in the hands of the customer, the better off you will be.

Don’t be a hero:

It’s better to be a quitter than be a hero. Don’t waste more time just because you have invested time.

Go to sleep:

Forgoing sleep is a bad idea. The costs of it include stubbornness, lack of creativity, diminished morale and irritability. We do not want this. Be at peace!

Your estimates suck:

While we all estimate how things will go, we all go wrong by huge margin. Like “Planning is guessing.”

Long lists don’t get done:

Break the long to-do list into smaller ones. Prioritize the list – put the most important thing on the top, and least important thing at the bottom.

Make tiny decisions:

Big decisions are hard to make and hard to change. Making tiny decisions doesn’t mean we don’t make big plans; it is just that we build on these tiny decisions to achieve big goals. Attainable goals are the best!

Competitors

Don’t copy:

When you are a student, imitation can be a helpful tool on the path to discovering your own voice. However, in business copying is a sure thing to failure. Be inspired. Don’t steal. If bulk of your work is same as others – its copy.

De-Commoditize your product:

If you are successful, people will copy – it’s a fact. But build your product with you – how you deliver, how you contact customers, how you resolve issues, etc. competitors cannot copy this you.

Pick a fight:

Having an enemy gives a great story to customers. Taking a stand always stands out.

Underdo your competition:

It is always possible that we aspire to do more than the competitor but underdoing and doing with better quality can help achieve loyal customer base. There are always customers who are not engaged either with you or your competitor, to bring them on board, the product should be minimal. Highlight it, sell it as aggressively as possible.

Who cares what they are doing?

Do not follow what your competitors are doing? Instead lead. What is the point if you are following what everybody else is doing? If you go down, do it while fighting for what you believe.

Evolution

Say no by default:

It’s easy to say yes. Avoids confrontation. Start saying no – even to your best ideas. Say no – people are surprisingly understanding when we take time to explain your point of view. The goal is to make your product stays right. It is you who believe the most of it. (If the product is you – saying no to other’s request is important because it’s you who believe the most who you want to become – make the best use of your time).

Let your customers outgrow you:

People and situations change. You must be true to a customer type rather than a customer with changing needs.

Don’t confuse enthusiasm with priority:

Great idea gives you a rush – we often drop everything and work towards this great idea. However, it is a bad idea. Park these ideas for some time and evaluate them later with calm mind.

Be at-home good:

Many products advertise with many promises but when the product is at home, it fails to deliver. This eliminates the customer from its base. However, if the product lives up to the expectations of the user or beats the expectations of the user, the customer usually talks about it to their friends too. You can’t paint a bad experience with good advertising and marketing.

Don’t write it down:

Writing down the customer feedback into a spreadsheet and evaluating them constantly is a waste of time. If it is important, we will be listening to them multiple times. That is when we should start working on it.

Promotion

Welcome obscurity:

Being in shadows allows you to make mistakes, try new things and is a great position to be in as there is nothing (like ego) at stake. Also, it doesn’t make sense to be seen by people when you are not ready to be seen.

Build an audience:

All companies have customers, some are fortunate to have fans but only few have audiences. Building an audience gives you a loyal customer base. We do not have to spend on ads, but the audience reach out to us.

Out-teach your competition:

Big companies can afford an ad at super bowl, but a small company cannot. Teaching forms a bond with the customers, if they like your teaching, they will probably like the product too. Also, Big companies prefer secrecy; teaching can be your chance to outmaneuver.

Emulate chefs:

Well known chefs usually put the recipes in cookbooks, TV shows, etc. Chefs understand that there is no one who will read the book and start a business next to him and that no one can beat the chefs at their own game.

Go Behind the scenes:

Let the people see what is happening behind the scenes. This will help people connect with you at a deeper level – as a human being who is putting efforts to create something instead of a faceless company.

Nobody likes plastic flowers:

The “professionals” who wears uniform try to seem perfect. However, people like the real flowers which wilt. The Japanese principle “Wabi Sabi” teaches that one should embrace the cracks and imperfections instead of the shiny façade. Show the latest version of what you are working, even though it is not finished. It may not seem professional, but it appears genuine.

Press releases are spam:

The generic pitch through the press release has no meaning as it is intended for thousands of journalists out there. Instead of doing the same thing (press release) that everybody does, give a personal touch to the journalist who had covered the similar article as yours. Be remarkable, be unforgettable.

Forget about the Wal Street journal:

The famous journals’ journalists are too busy for you. However, the fringe media journalists who are hungry for the fresh meat may be available on a phone call or respond to email quickly. Target these initially.

Drug dealers get it right:

Drug dealers know that their product is so good that they give away some for free. They know that you will come back with money. Create a product that is so good, so addictive. If you are not confident that customers will not return, then you have not created a strong product.

Marketing is not a department:

Do not consider marketing as a separate department. Because everything you do to please the customer, respond to an email, write an article on the website, etc. are all marketing. Marketing is 24/7/365. Marketing is not a department but the sum of everything we do.

The myth of overnight sensation:

Nobody becomes successful overnight. Trade the dream of overnight success to slow, measured growth. It’s hard, but you need to patient. Good PR won’t work either – at least you are not ready for it. Bottomline, be patient. Work right. Build audience.

Hiring

Do it yourself first:

Hiring is exciting as we welcome new members, but it should be based on need. More importantly, you should be able to do the job by yourself. Only, when necessary, one should hire. The advantage of doing it by yourself is that we know what we want. Else, we are solely dependent on others.

Hire when it hurts:

Don’t hire for pleasure; Hire to kill pain. When you know that you can’t take any more of the work, quality level is dropping, etc. that is when you should hire.

Pass on great people:

There are plenty of great people. We can pass on great people if we do not have the requirement as above (pain). Anything else would be like a dead stock.

Strangers at a cocktail party:

When you hire people, you do not know very well, or hire excessively, it would be strangers at a cocktail party who only do small talk, be polite to each other instead of being critical and hard discussions.

Resumes are ridiculous:

Resumes are full of action words that has become cliché. Instead, look for a personal story in cover letter.

Years of irrelevance:

Work experience years are overrated; however, people having at least six-month work experience is recommended as that gets acquaintance of the work lingo, culture, etc.

Forget about formal education:

Formal education makes student learn many things however, in professional world one needs to unlearn. For example, being formal in emails instead of conversational, etc. The pool of great candidates is bigger than people with better GPAs.

Everybody works:

Delegators are dead weight. Delegators love to pull people into meetings. Meanwhile everybody else is pulled away from getting real work done.

Hire managers of one:

Managers of one are people who come up with their own goals and execute them. Finding such people who is capable of building from scratch and seeing it through frees the rest of the team.

Hire great writers:

When there is a tie between two people, a person who writes better should be the winner. Better writing skills shows that the person is organized in thoughts, knows how to articulate, etc.

The best is everywhere:

Best people are across the world and the location should not be a hindrance in hiring. However, occasional in-person meeting should be mandatory to discuss progress, shortcomings, future, etc.

Test drive employees:

Interviews gives an initial estimate; however, people don’t seem to work as pro as they sound. It is therefore advised to hire after knowing their work. Create a mini project or prototype project and evaluate the person.

Damage Control

Own your bad news:

Hiding bad news is impossible. However, the narrative can be controlled. Speak upfront about the bad news, own it up, apologize, and work towards rectifying. If the message comes from the top, it has high credibility.

Speed changes everything:

Once you answer quickly and genuinely, people are more forgiving.

How to say you are sorry:

There’s never a great way to say sorry but there are terrible ways to say sorry. Avoid the stock apologies.

Put everyone on the front lines:

Everyone should have direct access to the customer comments. The people who are developing, the people are directly communicating with the customers and the people who are decision makers. Customer feedback can go far from reality as the number of hops increase. The raw feedback either positive or negatives motivates everyone towards excellence.

Take a deep breath:

Negative comments for a change are common; in fact, only it is heard. However, the perception or acceptance of the change occurs with time and the negative knee-jerk reactions subsides. One should abstain from reverting to original because of knee-jerk reactions while acknowledging and responding to the negative reactions with a calm mind.

Culture

You don’t create a culture:

Nobody creates a culture or give a culture book. It is the practices that go into culture book. Something can go into culture book, if and only if it is practiced every day.

Decisions are temporary:

Being small, there is an advantage of making small decisions and changing them in future. Always, decisions can be changed but a decision now is important to make progress.

Skip the rock stars:

Everyone is looking for rockstars. Rather create a rock star environment. A rock star environment can unlock the potential, which is otherwise trapped under lame policies, poor direction, stifling bureaucracies. Rock star environments develop out of trust, autonomy and responsibility.

They are not thirteen:

When you treat people like children, you get children’s work. When everything constantly needs approval, you create a culture of nonthinkers. Failing to trust employees is awfully expensive.

Send people home at 5:

The dream employee should not be the one who has no life outside the work. Such employee, aka, burn-the-midnight-oil isn’t great as they miss the point that “you don’t need more hours; you need better hours.” If you want something done, ask the busiest person. You shouldn’t expect the job to be someone’s entire life – at least not if you want to keep them around for a long time.

Don’t scar on the first cut:

When something goes wrong the first time, we should not create policy for the entire firm. This is punishing all the employees for the mistake of one. Create a policy only if something is happening often.

Sound like you:

Sounding big doesn’t make them appear bigger and more “professional.” Be you. Talk as a real person. Talk how you talk in day-to-day life.

Four-letter words:

Need, must, easy, just, only, fast. These words don’t convey the truth. These words make it hard to find a solution as they are demanding.

ASAP is poison:

When ASAP is used with everything, nothing that isn’t associated with ASAP can be done in time. Meaning, ASAP loses its value. Reserve your emergency language only for emergencies.

Conclusion

Inspiration is perishable:

We all have ideas, ideas are immortal. Inspiration is like fresh fruit or milk; it has an expiration date. Inspiration is a magical thing, a productivity multiplier, a motivator but with time limit. Put it to work.
